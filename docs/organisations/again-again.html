Organisation Overview

Again Again is a New Zealand company founded by Nada Piatek and Melissa Firth that operates a technology platform enabling cafes and other foodservice businesses to offer customers reusable, returnable containers instead of single-use cups and packaging. Customers pay a small deposit (historically NZ$3) for a reusable stainless-steel cup or container, which can be returned to any participating venue for a refund; venues access the system via a mobile app and dashboard. The company launched its pilot cup-lending scheme in Wellington around 2018 and has since partnered with local councils and community organisations to run reuse pilots in other New Zealand cities, including Hamilton and Hastings.

Business Model

Again Again's core model is B2B: it supplies participating cafes, food outlets and events with a fleet of reusable containers (primarily stainless-steel cups, expanding toward other container formats) and the supporting technology — a customer-facing app for borrowing/returning, and a vendor dashboard for inventory and transaction tracking. Consumers borrow a container with a small refundable deposit and return it to any participating venue in the network, rather than the original point of purchase, which is intended to make reuse as convenient as disposal. Revenue is generated from venue/vendor fees and deposit float rather than from consumers directly. The company has partnered with local councils (e.g., Hamilton City Council's Waste Minimisation Fund via Impact Hub Waikato, and Hastings District Council) to fund and run city-level reuse pilots, indicating a mixed commercial/grant-funded delivery model at the city level.

Circular Economy Activities

  • Reusable Packaging — fleet of reusable stainless-steel cups (and expanding container types) for cafes/foodservice
  • Deposit Return — refundable-deposit borrow-and-return model
  • Sharing — containers returnable to any participating venue, not just point of purchase
  • Digital Tracking — mobile app and vendor dashboard for loan/return and inventory management

Products & Services

Reusable cup/container lending-as-a-service for cafes and foodservice venues, including physical container fleets, a consumer mobile app, and a vendor management dashboard.

Customers

B2B: cafes, foodservice businesses, and (via local pilots) councils and community organisations in New Zealand; end consumers interact with the system when ordering takeaway drinks/food at participating venues.

Partnerships

  • Hamilton City Council (Waste Minimisation Fund) and Impact Hub Waikato — delivered a "Let's Reuse Hamilton" pilot (confirmed December 2023)
  • Hastings District Council — local reuse-cup scheme partner
  • PledgeMe — equity crowdfunding platform used for a public capital raise

Measurable Impact

The only figure independently verified in this research pass is localised and dated: the Hamilton pilot (delivered with Impact Hub Waikato and funded by Hamilton City Council) diverted "more than 2,000" disposable cups from landfill in its first six weeks, across five participating cafes, as reported in a Hamilton City Council article dated 20 December 2023. Broader, company-wide or national-scale figures (e.g., total cafes nationwide, total cups diverted annually) referenced in some secondary summaries could not be independently confirmed from a primary source in this pass and are recorded here as Not publicly available.

Technology used

Mobile app for consumers to borrow/return containers; vendor-facing dashboard for participating cafes to manage inventory and transactions.

REUSE Foundation Assessment

Again Again operates a genuine, purpose-built reuse infrastructure model — B2B reusable container lending with deposit-return and digital tracking — directly aligned with circular-economy goals for foodservice packaging. Verified impact data is currently limited to a single dated local pilot (Hamilton, six weeks, five cafes, 2,000+ cups); nationwide scale and cumulative impact were not independently confirmed in this pass. As a small New Zealand social enterprise partnering with local government to fund city-level rollouts, it is a credible and relevant reuse operator, but public documentation of its overall network size and total impact should be strengthened before making broad quantitative claims.
