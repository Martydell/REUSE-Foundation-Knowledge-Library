Organisation Overview

EcoU is a Singapore-based supplier of disposable cutlery, plates, bowls, cups, straws and takeaway packaging, marketed as an eco-friendlier alternative to plastic disposables. Its core products are FSC-certified plantation-birch wooden cutlery (compostable in around 90 days under ideal conditions) and sugarcane-bagasse tableware, which upcycles a sugar-industry by-product into biodegradable/compostable plates and bowls (compostable in as little as 60 days). The company serves both retail (online shop) and wholesale/commercial customers, including hospitality and food-service businesses, with customisation and retailer-partner programmes. No founding date, founder names, or ownership details were found in this research pass.

Business Model

EcoU sells single-use, compostable/biodegradable tableware manufactured from renewable and upcycled materials — birch wood and sugarcane bagasse — as a substitute for conventional plastic disposables, targeting both individual retail buyers (via its online store) and B2B food-service, hospitality and events customers through wholesale and customisation/private-label programmes. Its value proposition is "responsible convenience": products remain single-use and disposable, but are designed to biodegrade or compost within weeks to a few months rather than persisting as plastic waste, and are sourced from FSC-certified or upcycled agricultural by-product material rather than virgin plastic. This is fundamentally a materials-substitution business (compostable-for-plastic disposables) rather than a reuse, refill, or durable-goods business.

REUSE Foundation Assessment

EcoU does not operate a reuse, refill, or durable-goods model: its entire product range is explicitly single-use and disposable, distinguished only by using compostable/biodegradable materials (birch wood, sugarcane bagasse) instead of plastic. While this reduces plastic pollution and may be a legitimate sustainability improvement over conventional disposables, it runs counter to the reuse hierarchy the Foundation prioritises (reduce/reuse before recycle/compost) and does not fit any of the Foundation's defined circular-economy activity categories for reuse operators. This profile is included for completeness per the research brief, but the organisation has low relevance to a reuse-focused knowledge library.
