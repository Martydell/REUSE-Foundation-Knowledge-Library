Organisation Overview

GreenChar is a Kenyan social enterprise founded in December 2013 by Tom Osborn, who was 17–18 years old at the time and living in a rural area affected by household air pollution from wood-fuel cooking (his mother was diagnosed with a wood-smoke-related respiratory infection). GreenChar produces smokeless, high-energy fuel briquettes — sold under the brand "MakaaPoa" for household jikos (cookstoves) and "Long Burn" for institutional/industrial use — made by compressing agricultural waste such as sugarcane pulp (sourced via Nzoia Sugar Factory) and cassava peelings, working with local women's and youth groups for waste collection and briquette production. Osborn received early recognition including a Forbes 30 Under 30 listing and an Echoing Green Fellowship.

Business Model

GreenChar sources agricultural waste — primarily sugarcane pulp/bagasse from Nzoia Sugar Factory and cassava peelings — from local women's and youth groups, who are paid to collect and supply the feedstock. This waste is processed and compressed into two branded briquette products: "MakaaPoa," a smokeless, long-burning, high-energy fuel for household jikos (charcoal stoves), and "Long Burn," positioned as a cost-effective industrial/institutional fuel alternative. The company's value proposition rests on displacing conventional wood charcoal, which the company states its briquettes outperform by emitting roughly 90% less smoke and providing about 60% more energy. GreenChar operated as an early-stage social enterprise funded substantially through grants and fellowship support (Echoing Green Fellowship contributing a reported $80,000 of a total $90,000+ raised) rather than through commercial equity investment, consistent with typical clean-cooking-sector social enterprises in East Africa.

REUSE Foundation Assessment

GreenChar is best understood as an alternative-fuel and agricultural-waste-valorisation enterprise rather than a "reuse" business in the packaging/product-reuse sense central to most of this knowledge library. Its core activity — converting agricultural waste into combustible briquettes — is more accurately classified as material recovery/waste-to-fuel conversion than reuse, reuse packaging, or refill systems, and it does not involve reusable products, containers, or take-back logistics. It should be retained in the library only as an adjacent/comparison case (illustrating agricultural-waste circularity in the Global South) rather than a core reuse-economy example, and the "Other" category assignment above should be understood as a deliberate flag rather than an oversight. Public documentation is also dated, concentrated in 2014–2016 press coverage of founder Tom Osborn's early recognition, with no strong evidence found of GreenChar's current (2026) operational status.

Priority: ★★ Low
