Organisation Overview

Bamboo Straw Girl is a Singapore-based social enterprise founded in 2013 by Melissa Lam, who began distributing bamboo straws to family, friends and drinks-stall vendors after being struck by the irony of using single-use plastics during camping trips in nature. Described as Singapore's first "zero-waste lifestyle" store, predating the wider popularisation of the "zero-waste" term locally, the venture formally incorporated by 2018 and now supplies reusable, locally made lifestyle goods to resorts, hotels and lifestyle stores internationally, alongside its own retail and educational activities.

Business Model

Bamboo Straw Girl operates as a social enterprise retailer, selling reusable and low-waste lifestyle products — bamboo straws and related zero-waste goods — direct to consumers via its own online store and, since scaling into an incorporated company by 2018, wholesale to resorts, hotels and lifestyle retailers regionally and internationally. A core part of its model is sourcing from and supporting independent Southeast Asian makers and artisans, providing them full-time employment or supplementary income through production of the goods it sells — combining a retail business with an explicit maker-livelihood mission. Alongside product sales, the company runs consumer-education activities, including public speaking engagements in Singapore aimed at spreading "mindful consumption" and zero-waste messaging, and its founder has publicly engaged government on strengthening national plastic-waste policy.

Circular Economy Activities

  • Reusable Packaging (bamboo straws and other reusable lifestyle goods replacing single-use plastic equivalents)
  • Waste Reduction (verified claim of preventing over 3 million single-use plastic items, and giving over 7,000 packaging items a second use)
  • Consumer Education (public speaking engagements reaching over 10,000 people in Singapore; social-media conscious-consumption messaging)

Not identified from public sources: refill systems, deposit-return schemes, rental/sharing models, or reverse-logistics/take-back programmes.

Products & Services

Bamboo straws and other reusable, locally made zero-waste lifestyle products (e.g. household and personal-care reusable goods), sold via direct online retail and wholesale to resorts, hotels and lifestyle stores.

Customers

Individual consumers in Singapore and internationally via e-commerce; wholesale customers including resorts, hotels and lifestyle retail stores; participants in the company's public education/speaking engagements.

Partnerships

Not publicly available as named formal commercial partnerships; the company works with "half a dozen" (approximately six) independent Southeast Asian makers and artisans as production partners/suppliers, per its own impact page, though these makers are not individually named in verified public sources reviewed.

Measurable Impact

  • Over 3 million single-use plastic items prevented (Bamboo Straw Girl, "Our Impact" page, https://bamboostrawgirl.com/pages/our-impact, cumulative to date of publication)
  • Over 7,000 packaging items given a second use (same source)
  • More than 10,000 people educated through in-person speaking engagements in Singapore (same source)
  • Approximately half a dozen Southeast Asian makers/artisans supported through full-time employment or supplementary income (same source)

Technology

Not publicly available; operations are described as direct retail (online store) and wholesale distribution rather than a proprietary digital platform.

REUSE Foundation Assessment

Bamboo Straw Girl is a well-documented, long-running (since 2013) Singapore social enterprise with genuine, self-reported impact metrics (millions of single-use items prevented, thousands of people educated) and a clear maker-livelihood mission supporting independent Southeast Asian artisans — a strong example of grassroots reuse-product retail combined with consumer education. Its self-reported impact figures are sourced from the company's own "Our Impact" page rather than independently audited third-party verification, which should be noted, but the company's longevity, social-enterprise structure and consistent public messaging make it a credible and relevant case study for the REUSE Foundation library.
