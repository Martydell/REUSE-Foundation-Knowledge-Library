KOKO Networks

A Nairobi-based clean-cooking company that distributed refillable bioethanol fuel via smart-canister "Fuel ATM" stations across East Africa — now in wind-down/administration.

Organisation Overview

KOKO Networks was a Nairobi-headquartered clean-cooking company that distributed bioethanol cooking fuel as a substitute for charcoal, sold through a network of ATM-like "KOKO Point" fuel-dispensing stations. Customers used a reusable, chip-enabled "KOKO Canister" paired with a KOKO Cooker (stove) to refill bioethanol fuel repeatedly rather than purchasing new containers. At its peak (mid-2023) the company reported roughly 1 million household customers across eight Kenyan cities plus Rwanda, and employed approximately 1,800 people. In 2026, KOKO announced it was closing after the Kenyan government failed to authorise carbon-credit sales and bioethanol import permits the business depended on; PwC was appointed as administrator on February 1, 2026, and company assets went up for sale.

Business Model

KOKO Networks' model combined a hardware distribution network with a subscription-like fuel-refill service. Customers purchased a low-cost starter kit (cooker plus fuel canister, reported around $15 in 2022) and then repeatedly refilled the reusable smart canister at unattended, ATM-style "KOKO Point" fuel stations located in dense urban neighbourhoods — avoiding the need to buy new packaging or containers for each fuel purchase. The company operated roughly 700 fuel-dispensing machines by 2019, expanding into Rwanda in 2022 via a partnership with Dalberg Ventures. Bioethanol supply came from imports and a partnership with Vivo Energy using sugarcane-processing waste; cooking stoves were manufactured in India. Crucially, KOKO's low customer pricing was subsidised (reportedly over $100 million in subsidies) through revenue from selling carbon credits internationally (including to South Korea, with planned expansion to Singapore/Japan) and backed by a $179.6 million political-risk guarantee from the World Bank Group's MIGA (March 2025). This carbon-credit-dependent subsidy model became unviable when the Kenyan government did not issue required Letters of Authorisation for international carbon-credit sales and also restricted bioethanol import permits, precipitating the company's 2026 closure.

Circular Economy Activities

  • Refill Systems — reusable, chip-enabled bioethanol fuel canisters refilled repeatedly at unattended dispensing stations
  • Reverse Logistics — centralised bioethanol supply and distribution network feeding neighbourhood-level refill points
  • Consumer Education — promotion of clean-cooking fuel as a health/deforestation-reducing alternative to charcoal

Products & Services

KOKO Cooker (bioethanol stove), reusable smart KOKO Canister (fuel container), and bioethanol fuel sold/refilled via "KOKO Point" ATM-style fuel-dispensing stations.

Customers

Urban households in Kenya (reported across eight cities) and Rwanda, reported at approximately 1 million household customers as of August 2023; sold primarily to lower- and middle-income urban consumers switching from charcoal.

Partnerships

  • Vivo Energy — bioethanol supply partnership (sugarcane-processing waste)
  • Dalberg Ventures — partnership supporting 2022 Rwanda market expansion
  • Multilateral Investment Guarantee Agency (MIGA, World Bank Group) — $179.6 million political risk guarantee (March 2025)
  • PwC — appointed as administrator for wind-down (February 2026)
  • Carbon credit buyers in South Korea (with stated plans for Singapore and Japan)

Measurable Impact

Approximately 1 million household customers reported as of August 2023 (company-reported); approximately 700 fuel-dispensing "KOKO Point" machines operating as of 2019; approximately 1,800 employees as of mid-2023 (Wikipedia, sourced from company/press reporting). No independently audited lifetime CO2, deforestation-avoidance, or plastic-reduction figures were found; carbon-credit volumes underlying the subsidy model were disputed/unauthorised by Kenyan authorities, contributing to the 2026 closure.

Technology

Chip/electronically-enabled "smart" bioethanol canisters linked to unattended, ATM-style fuel-dispensing "KOKO Point" stations for cashless/metered refuelling.

REUSE Foundation Assessment

KOKO Networks was a genuinely large-scale, technology-enabled refill system — its reusable canister/fuel-ATM model reached roughly a million households and represented one of the most significant reuse-based clean-cooking deployments in East Africa. However, its 2026 closure (confirmed via company statements, Wikipedia, and press reporting) following the collapse of its carbon-credit-dependent subsidy model means the operation is no longer active, and its infrastructure/assets are being sold off. It remains highly relevant as a case study in reuse-system scaling and the fragility of carbon-finance-subsidised reuse business models, but is not an ongoing operating example for the REUSE Foundation to highlight as active.

Verification Notes

Seed data listed founding year as 2012; verified sources (Wikipedia, KfW) consistently state 2014 as the founding/launch year, with one secondary source citing a 2013 founding by CEO Greg Murray — 2012 was not corroborated by any source found and appears to be an error in the seed data. Seed also listed Uganda as an operating country; verified sources confirm Kenya and Rwanda as operating markets but did not confirm active Uganda operations — this should be treated as unconfirmed. Critically, the company's 2026 closure/administration (PwC appointed February 1, 2026) is a major, well-sourced recent development not reflected in the seed data and materially changes the organisation's current status from "active" to "winding down."
