Organisation Overview

EcoEnclose was founded in 2010 by Erin Kimmett, initially to supply sustainable packaging for her own cloth-diaper company, Thirsties, before growing into a standalone sustainable packaging business. In 2015, current owners Saloni Doshi and Kyle Wente acquired the company, scaling it from three employees in a 2,000 sq ft Louisville, Colorado warehouse into a Certified B Corporation (score 94.3, top 10% globally for environmental performance) serving more than 25,000 business customers of all sizes with recycled and sustainable e-commerce shipping packaging.

Business Model

EcoEnclose operates a B2B model supplying sustainable shipping and packaging materials — mailers, boxes, tape, and void-fill — made from 100% recycled or otherwise sustainable materials, to e-commerce and retail businesses ranging from small independent brands to larger companies. Its value proposition centres on making sustainable packaging accessible and customisable (including branded/printed options) without requiring customers to compromise on functionality or cost compared to conventional packaging. The company is Climate Neutral Certified and a Certified B Corporation, embedding third-party-audited environmental and social governance standards into its operating model, and it has publicly committed to transparency and industry-wide knowledge sharing (e.g., publishing sustainability data and practices for the wider packaging industry rather than treating them as proprietary advantage).

REUSE Foundation Assessment

EcoEnclose is a well-documented, independently verified sustainable packaging business with strong third-party validation: B Corp certification with a top-decile environmental score, Climate Neutral Certified status, and substantial independent press coverage (Inc. Magazine, CompanyWeek, Packaging Strategies). Its scale (25,000+ B2B customers, 47 million pounds of waste diverted) is unusually well-quantified compared to most organisations in this batch. However, its core offering is recycled/sustainable single-use packaging materials rather than a reusable packaging loop or take-back system — it reduces the environmental footprint of packaging rather than eliminating single-use packaging through reuse, which is a relevant distinction for REUSE Foundation's specific reuse-system focus.
