Kamupak

Finnish reusable-takeaway-packaging pioneer that merged with Sweden's &Repeat in 2023 to form a 500+ customer Nordic leader — then announced it was closing down in July 2024.

Organisation Overview

Kamupak was a Finnish reusable-takeaway-packaging company founded in 2018 (some sources say 2019) to combat single-use plastic food packaging via a digital deposit-return system. Its flagship product, the KamuDish (a 1.2-litre reusable flat container), launched in 2020 and by 2021 had replaced 10,000 single-use containers across 50 restaurants, cafés, and supermarkets in Finland. In August 2023, Kamupak merged with Stockholm-based competitor &Repeat to form a combined Nordic circular-packaging leader with 500+ customers across Sweden and Finland, including major brands like Pressbyrån, Espresso House, Wolt, Sodexo, Kespro, and Picnic — but the company announced it was closing down in July 2024, citing an inability to resolve its financial situation.

Business Model

Kamupak operated a digital deposit-return system for reusable takeaway food containers: customers paid a deposit when receiving food in a KamuDish container, then returned it (via participating locations) to reclaim the deposit, with Kamupak managing the collection/cleaning/redistribution loop for restaurant and retail clients. Following the 2023 merger with &Repeat (forming a combined entity under Kamupak CEO Ilkka Puikkonen), the strategy shifted toward AI and advanced RFID technology to reduce friction for both consumers and takeaway operators. Despite reaching meaningful scale (500+ customers, major brand clients) post-merger, the combined business proved financially unsustainable and ceased operations in July 2024.

REUSE Foundation Assessment

Kamupak is a highly instructive case study precisely because of its trajectory: genuine early traction (10,000 containers, 50 locations by 2021), a scale-building merger (500+ customers across two countries by 2023), and then complete closure within roughly a year of that merger (July 2024) — illustrating that even well-funded, seemingly successful reuse-system consolidation does not guarantee financial sustainability. Highly relevant as a cautionary/lessons-learned case for the REUSE Foundation library.

Priority Rating: ★★★★ High (high relevance as a documented failure case, despite the company itself no longer operating)
