Organisation Overview

Let's Get Naked Refill is a small independent refill shop located in the White Hart Shopping Arcade in Launceston, Cornwall. Founded by Jade Harvey after being furloughed from a cafe job during the pandemic, the shop lets customers bring their own containers (or use provided paper bags) to buy pantry staples such as pasta, rice, lentils and pulses, alongside refillable shampoo, conditioner and other health and beauty products. It represents a very small-scale, community-level example of the package-free retail model rather than a scaled or multi-site operation.

Business Model

The shop operates a straightforward bring-your-own-container retail model: customers weigh empty containers, fill them with bulk goods (dry pantry staples, cleaning products, health and beauty refills), and pay by weight or volume, eliminating single-use packaging at the point of sale. It is a single-location, owner-operated small business rather than a franchise or multi-site chain, started with the backing of a SWIG Finance start-up loan and business-planning support from the Cornwall & Isles of Scilly Growth Hub's Access to Finance service. The shop sources from ethical and environmentally conscious suppliers and positions itself as a local sustainable-shopping alternative to supermarket packaged goods, citing examples such as a jar of Italian herbs costing 30p refilled versus £1.45 for an equivalent new packaged jar at a supermarket. It has explored expanding into click-and-collect and online ordering. There is no public evidence of external investment, multi-site expansion, or corporate partnerships; it remains a single high-street shop serving its local Launceston community.

REUSE Foundation Assessment

Let's Get Naked Refill is a genuine, verifiable example of grassroots refill retail, but it is a single small shop with no evident scale, funding, or replicable model beyond its own premises. It is useful as a small-scale case study of community-level reuse retail and consumer behaviour change, but it offers limited insight into scalable circular-economy infrastructure, has no measurable published impact data, and no corporate or NGO partnerships were found. It should be catalogued as a micro-enterprise example rather than a sector-shaping organisation.

Priority: ★★ Low (legitimate small-scale refill shop; limited public data, no scale or partnerships to assess broader sector relevance)
