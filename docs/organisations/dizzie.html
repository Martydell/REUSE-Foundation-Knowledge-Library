Dizzie

UK reusable-packaging-as-a-service provider for grocery brands and retailers, formerly the D2C zero-waste grocer Good Club, which ceased trading and entered voluntary liquidation in November 2024.

Organisation Overview

Dizzie was a UK reusable-packaging company, legally registered as Good Clubs Limited, founded by Ben Patten. It began in 2019 as Good Club, a direct-to-consumer online grocer delivering products in reusable containers that were collected, washed, and refilled. In September 2022 the company rebranded as Dizzie and pivoted to a B2B "reusable packaging as a service" model, supplying empty and filled reusable containers, collection, washing, and tracking services to grocers and brands. It closed its direct-to-consumer store in March 2023 to focus on B2B. The company ceased operations and entered voluntary liquidation in November 2024, with founder Ben Patten citing stalled grocer adoption and an inability to raise further investment.

Business Model

Good Club began as a direct-to-consumer "zero-waste supermarket" launched in 2019: customers ordered grocery staples online, received them in reusable pots, and returned the empty packaging with their next delivery for washing and refilling. Following a September 2022 rebrand to Dizzie, the company repositioned as a B2B infrastructure provider, offering brands and retailers a "plug and play" reusable-packaging service — supplying reusable containers, filling and labelling support, collection logistics, industrial washing, and (per a 2022 funding round announcement) a reusable-container tracking system, without retailers needing to build their own reuse operations. It worked with independent retailers and grocers before securing partnerships with larger online grocers, at one point supplying three national grocers reaching an estimated 2 million households weekly (per the company's own account of its history). It raised over £500,000 in a December 2022 crowdfunding round intended to fund a trial with a major grocer, roll out container tracking, and develop new packaging formats. The company ceased direct-to-consumer sales in March 2023, continued as a B2B supplier through 2023–2024, and ceased operations entirely in November 2024, entering creditors'/members' voluntary liquidation.

Products & Services

Reusable, stackable/nestable grocery containers (multiple design generations: an early stackable HDPE format, and a later biocomposite — wood fibre and bio-polypropylene — format designed with Blond in 2022, rated for repeated reuse cycles); filling and labelling of containers for brand partners; collection, industrial washing, and redistribution ("reverse logistics") of returned packaging; a reusable-container tracking system for brand/retailer partners.

Customers

Online and bricks-and-mortar grocery retailers and food/drink brands in the UK. Named partners included Abel & Cole (its "Club Zero" reusable range), Milk & More, and The Modern Milkman. At peak, the company reported supplying three national online grocers reaching roughly 2 million households per week (approximately 50% of UK households), per the company's own retrospective account — not independently audited.

Measurable Impact

Figures below are self-reported by the company (its own Medium retrospective, published around its 2024 closure, and earlier press coverage of its December 2022 crowdfunding round) and have not been independently audited; no year-by-year breakdown is given, these are cumulative totals across the company's full 2019–2024 existence unless noted:

  • Over 3 million reusable packaging units processed (company retrospective, medium.com/@goodclub)
  • 60.6 tonnes of waste removed; over 1.6 million units saved from landfill; associated emissions cut by 212 tonnes (company retrospective)
  • By early 2023: 3 national online grocers as clients, reaching approximately 2 million households weekly (~50% of UK households) (company retrospective)
  • Abel & Cole's "Club Zero" reuse range (supplied by Dizzie) improved Abel & Cole's customer retention by more than 2 percentage points, per the company's own account
  • A separate, earlier PA Consulting client-story piece (for an HDPE container redesign, not the full company) cites projected/self-reported figures of "over 1 million pieces of plastic saved annually" and "139,000+ kg of carbon emissions reduced per year" — these appear to relate to a specific container design exercise rather than total company impact, and are not corroborated elsewhere.

A third-party marketing/directory source (plasticfree.com) cites additional figures (e.g. £3 million annual revenue, 20 million units/month production capacity, RFID/"ReuseOS" branding, £666,590 crowdfunding total) that could not be corroborated against primary sources (Companies House, The Grocer, the company's own retrospective) and are therefore excluded from this profile as unverifiable.

Technology

Dizzie stated in 2022 (via press coverage of its crowdfunding round) that it intended to use new funding to "roll out its reusable container tracking system." No primary source reviewed specifies the underlying technology (e.g. RFID, QR/barcode, or a named software platform) in a corroborated way. Not publicly available beyond this general description.

Challenges

Founder Ben Patten attributed the company's November 2024 closure to stalled adoption of reusable packaging among grocers and an inability to attract further investment to scale the business, describing the company as "perhaps, just too early." The company's own retrospective additionally cites macro headwinds — the UK cost-of-living crisis, reduced venture funding availability, and delays to UK Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) policy — as contributing factors. It also notes that partner Milk & More was acquired by Freshways during 2023–2024, which reversed that particular reuse arrangement.

REUSE Foundation Assessment

Dizzie is a directly relevant case study for the REUSE Foundation library: a UK reusable-packaging-as-a-service operator that ran a genuine, multi-year, multi-retailer reuse loop (container supply, fill, collection, wash, redistribution, and tracking) rather than a materials-substitution or recycling model. Its self-reported scale (3M+ units processed, national-grocer partnerships reaching an estimated ~50% of UK households) and its documented, named commercial partnerships (Abel & Cole, Milk & More, The Modern Milkman) make it a useful real-world proof point on both the potential and the fragility of reuse-as-a-service business models. Its 2024 closure — attributed by its founder to stalled grocer adoption, funding constraints, and premature market timing — is itself a valuable, well-documented failure case for understanding barriers to scaling reuse infrastructure in UK grocery retail.

Priority Rating: ★★★★ High (directly on-scope reuse/refill infrastructure model with verifiable named partners and a well-documented closure narrative, despite being no longer operating)
