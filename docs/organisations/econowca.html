Orange County's first zero-waste shop and bulk refill station, founded 2018, offering package-free home, health and beauty refills alongside handmade reusable goods across a network of Southern California storefronts.

Organisation Overview

Eco Now is a Southern California zero-waste retailer and bulk refill shop, founded in 2018 by Thea Merritt, initially selling hand-sewn reusable alternatives to single-use plastics at a farmers' market in Placentia, California. It grew into brick-and-mortar stores across Orange County and the Inland Empire — Anaheim, Costa Mesa, Laguna Beach and Riverside were all opened at various points between 2018 and 2021 — offering fill-up refill stations for household cleaning, personal care and hygiene products plus handmade, package-free goods such as mesh produce bags, beeswax wraps and paper-towel alternatives.

Business Model

Eco Now operates a direct-to-consumer retail model combining (a) bulk refill stations, where customers bring or purchase reusable containers and pay by weight/volume for products such as shampoo, home-cleaning liquids and bulk foods, and (b) sale of handmade and sourced package-free/reusable goods (produce bags, food-storage wraps, bulk-food bags). Revenue comes from in-store retail sales at its Southern California locations and via online ordering. The company has both a physical-retail and community/education component, offering in-store composting education and upcycling assistance. As of 2025–2026, the business has been consolidating: the Laguna Beach location (opened 2022) closed around September 1, 2025, the Riverside location is listed as closed, and the Costa Mesa flagship at The LAB Anti-Mall was reported temporarily closed while expanding to add package-free organic grocery alongside its refill and beauty offerings, with reopening referenced for 2026/2027. The Anaheim location (opened 2020) continues to be listed as operating in sources reviewed. Company size is reported by aggregator data as 11–50 employees (unverified, not from a primary company disclosure).

Circular Economy Activities

  • Refill Systems — bulk fill-up stations for home cleaning, personal care and hygiene products
  • Reusable Packaging — sale of reusable, handmade alternatives to single-use items (produce bags, food wraps, bulk-food bags)
  • Consumer Education — in-store composting education and upcycling assistance
  • Waste Reduction — core mission framed around helping customers "reduce unnecessary waste"

Products & Services

Bulk refill stations for shampoo, home cleaning products and hygiene items; handmade and sourced package-free goods including mesh produce bags, reusable bulk-food bags, beeswax food wraps and paper-towel alternatives; in-store composting and upcycling education/assistance; online store.

Customers

Southern California consumers (Orange County and, formerly, Inland Empire) seeking zero-waste and package-free household, personal-care and grocery alternatives.

Challenges

Multiple location closures and consolidation are documented in third-party business listings and local coverage: the Riverside store (opened June 2021, first zero-waste store in the Inland Empire) is listed as closed; the Laguna Beach store (opened 2022) closed around September 1, 2025, with the company citing a decision to narrow to fewer, more manageable locations while balancing family life; the Costa Mesa flagship was reported temporarily closed pending an expansion to add grocery offerings, with reopening dates cited variously as 2026/2027 in aggregator listings. These changes suggest the business has faced sustainability/scaling challenges in maintaining multiple independent physical storefronts.

REUSE Foundation Assessment

Eco Now is a genuine grassroots refill and zero-waste retail operation with a founder-driven, community-rooted model dating to 2018, and its core activity — bulk refill stations plus reusable-goods retail — sits squarely within the REUSE Foundation's focus areas. However, it is a small independent business with no publicly disclosed impact metrics, no confirmed corporate/NGO partnerships, and clear evidence of operational contraction (multiple store closures/consolidation in 2025). Its relevance lies in illustrating the challenges small independent refill retailers face in sustaining multi-location physical footprints, more than in providing a scalable or well-documented case study.

Priority Rating: ★★★ Medium
