Organisation Overview

Bottle Factory is a Seoul-based social enterprise founded in 2016 by CEO Jeong Da-woon, beginning as a reusable-cup café concept ("Bottle Café") in Yeonhui-dong before establishing its current base in Seodaemun-gu. The organisation operates a ground-floor café known as "Bottle Lounge," which serves no disposable cups (lending tumblers to customers without their own, and discounting drinks for customers who bring bottles from home), alongside a basement "Bottle Factory" area that has run a packaging-free detergent refill station since 2018. The company describes its mission as designing services and content to test practical, everyday solutions to environmental problems such as single-use packaging.

Business Model

Bottle Factory combines a retail café with an experimental refill-retail operation and a social-enterprise content/education practice. Its café ("Bottle Lounge") generates revenue from food and drink sales while eliminating disposable cups through reusable tumbler lending and price incentives for customers bringing their own containers. Its refill station ("채우장") sells detergents and cleaning agents — including New Zealand eco-brand Ecostore's laundry products, plus baking soda, sodium percarbonate, and organic fruit soap — dispensed package-free into customer-supplied containers. Beyond direct retail, the company describes a "Bottle Lab" function for experimenting with and validating new sustainability-oriented service concepts, and produces content examining questions such as the real-world outcomes of recycling and packaging-free consumption. As an accredited social enterprise, its structure emphasises social/environmental mission alongside commercial retail operations rather than pursuing rapid national scale-up.

REUSE Foundation Assessment

Bottle Factory is a genuine, long-running (since 2016) small-scale social enterprise combining a disposable-cup-free café with a package-free detergent refill station, representing a grassroots, community-level model of reuse retail in South Korea. Its accredited social-enterprise status and multi-year operating history lend it credibility, though its public documentation is limited mostly to Korean-language local/lifestyle media rather than formal impact reporting, and no quantified impact metrics were found. It is a useful case study of neighbourhood-scale refill/reuse retail but has modest scale and limited English-language sourcing.
