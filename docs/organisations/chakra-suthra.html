Organisation Overview

Chakra Suthra ("Chakra" = circularity, "Suthra" = formulas) is a Sri Lankan circular-economy company whose flagship programme, Trash2Cash, operates reverse-vending machines that accept empty plastic bottles, aluminium cans, and Tetra Paks, rewarding depositors via a mobile app or at partner retailers. Founded and led by Himesh Fernando, the company also offers circular-economy consulting and business-model re-engineering services. Its stated aim is to make homes and businesses in Sri Lanka zero-waste by keeping materials in circulation.

Business Model

Chakra Suthra's core public-facing activity is Trash2Cash: smart collection machines placed in accessible locations that accept plastic bottles, cans, and Tetra Paks in exchange for redeemable rewards via a companion mobile app or at selected retail partners. The company has partnered with Nestlé Lanka on the initiative and has trained micro-entrepreneurs — reported as predominantly women — to run collection operations at a grassroots level, giving them a new income source. Beyond the consumer-facing collection network, Chakra Suthra separately offers B2B circular-economy consulting and business-model re-engineering services to other organisations, positioning itself as both an operator and an advisor within Sri Lanka's circular economy.

Trash2Cash reverse-vending machines and companion mobile app; circular-economy consulting and business-model re-engineering services for other organisations.

Measurable Impact

Ten micro-entrepreneurs trained to lead collection operations, 70% of them women (PLEASE Project, undated). Sri Lanka's broader plastics-recycling rate cited as ~4% (context statistic, not a Chakra Suthra-specific metric). No independently audited volume/tonnage figures for Chakra Suthra itself were found.

Technology

Smart reverse-vending machines (Trash2Cash) with mobile-app-based reward redemption.

REUSE Foundation Assessment

Chakra Suthra is a genuine material-recovery/recycling-incentive operator rather than a reuse system in the strict sense (it collects materials for recycling rather than closing a reuse loop), but its reverse-logistics infrastructure and grassroots micro-entrepreneur model are relevant to circular-economy practice in an emerging-market context with very low baseline recycling rates. Its B2B consulting arm adds some relevance as a knowledge resource for other organisations building circular systems.
