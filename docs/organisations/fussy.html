Organisation Overview

Fussy is a UK company that makes a refillable natural deodorant designed to eliminate single-use plastic from the bathroom. Founded in 2020 and launched via a record-breaking Kickstarter campaign, the brand sells a reusable outer case paired with compostable cardboard-and-paper refill cartridges. Fussy has since expanded into refillable body wash and hand wash. It is a certified B Corporation and ships direct-to-consumer to more than 30 countries, positioning itself as a plastic-free, "clean" alternative to conventional stick deodorants and personal-care products sold in single-use plastic.

Business Model

Fussy operates a direct-to-consumer e-commerce and subscription model. Customers buy a durable, reusable plastic-free case once, then subscribe to receive replacement deodorant refill cartridges (made from compostable materials) roughly every 4-6 weeks. The subscription reduces per-unit packaging versus buying a new plastic stick deodorant each cycle, and used cartridges are designed to be compostable rather than requiring take-back. The company launched via Kickstarter in October 2020, raising over £114,000 and becoming one of the platform's highest-grossing deodorant campaigns, which provided both funding and an initial customer base. It later secured investment from Dragons' Den investors Deborah Meaden and Peter Jones. Fussy has broadened its product range beyond deodorant to include refillable body wash and hand wash, all sold under the same reusable-case-plus-refill logic, and markets itself heavily on sustainability credentials (B Corp certification, plastic-reduction claims) alongside product efficacy messaging aimed at consumers skeptical of "greenwashed" natural personal-care brands.

REUSE Foundation Assessment

Fussy is a legitimate, verifiable refill-based personal-care brand with a clear circular design (reusable case, replaceable compostable cartridge) and independent third-party validation via B Corp certification and Kickstarter/media coverage. Its impact is real but modest in scale relative to major FMCG packaging volumes, and its refill model relies on compostability of the cartridge rather than closed-loop reuse of the refill itself, which is a slightly different mechanism than deposit-return or industrial reuse systems the Foundation prioritises most highly. It remains a good example of consumer-facing refill innovation in personal care.
