## Organisation Overview

The Amore Store Gwanggyo Refill Station is a cosmetics refill concept launched by Amorepacific Corporation, South Korea's largest cosmetics company, in October 2020 — described in company and trade press as the first refill station in Korea's cosmetics industry. Located within the experiential "Amore Store Gwanggyo" retail concept store, it allows customers to dispense shampoo and body wash from any of 15 product lines into refillable containers made partly from coconut-shell material, paying by weight (per gram) at roughly a 50% discount to pre-packaged equivalents. Amorepacific expanded the format to a second store (Amore Store Hair & Body, E-mart Jayang) in May 2021, and government/industry commentary has cited the Gwanggyo store as a resource-recycling case study.

## Business Model

The Refill Station operates as an in-store retail feature within Amorepacific's broader "Amore Store" experiential retail format rather than a standalone company: customers bring or purchase a reusable dispenser container (available in multiple sizes, made using recycled/coconut-shell-derived material) and fill it with their chosen shampoo or body wash product from among 15 formulations on offer, with the product priced by weight rather than by fixed unit, and containers disinfected via UV-LED sterilisation before each refill for hygiene. Amorepacific frames the model as serving two purposes commercially: reducing packaging costs (passed to consumers as a roughly 50% price discount versus packaged equivalents) and generating footfall/brand engagement at flagship experiential stores, where the Gwanggyo location also offers custom cosmetics formulation and health-supplement consultation services. The company reported that content sold through the station is limited to product batches manufactured within the prior 100 days, a freshness/quality-control constraint specific to the bulk-refill format. As an initiative embedded within a large, diversified public cosmetics group, the Refill Station is not independently financed or reported — it is a retail-format pilot within Amorepacific's broader circular-packaging and sustainability strategy, alongside separate group-level initiatives such as its "Amorepacific Recycling Center" bottle take-back programme.

## Circular Economy Activities

- Refill Systems (core model: bring-a-container, pay-by-weight dispensing of shampoo/body wash)
- Reusable Packaging (dispenser containers made from recycled/coconut-shell-derived material, designed for repeated refill)
- Consumer Education (in-store experiential format introducing refill shopping to mainstream cosmetics consumers)
- Waste Reduction (reduced single-use bottle consumption per refill transaction)

## Products & Services

In-store refill dispensing of 15 shampoo and body wash formulations, sold by weight (10ml increments per Korean-language trade coverage) into reusable dispenser containers available in multiple sizes; UV-LED container sterilisation between refills; complementary experiential retail services at Amore Store Gwanggyo, including custom cosmetics formulation and health-supplement consultation.

## Customers

Individual retail consumers visiting Amorepacific's flagship experiential "Amore Store" locations in South Korea (Gwanggyo and E-mart Jayang/"Hair & Body" store); reported as attracting more than 1,000 refill purchasers in the months following the October 2020 launch.

## Partnerships

Not publicly available as named external commercial partners; this is an internally operated Amorepacific Corporation retail initiative. Korean government/industry bodies (e.g. a carbon-neutrality committee, per Pharmnews coverage) have cited the Gwanggyo store as a recognised resource-recycling case study, but this is a citation/recognition rather than an operating partnership.

## REUSE Foundation Assessment

The Amore Store Gwanggyo Refill Station is a well-documented, credibly sourced "first-of-its-kind" refill pilot from a major, publicly listed Asian cosmetics company, making it a useful example of large-incumbent (rather than startup) engagement with the refill model — showing that mainstream FMCG/cosmetics majors can operationalise pay-by-weight refill retail within existing store formats. However, verified impact data is thin and dated (a single "1,000+ customers" figure from early 2021, with no more recent public metrics located), and the initiative appears to remain a small-scale, two-location pilot within a vastly larger company rather than a scaled programme — its relevance should be understood as an illustrative case study of incumbent refill adoption rather than a major reuse-volume contributor.
