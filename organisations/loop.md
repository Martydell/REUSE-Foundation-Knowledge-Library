# Loop

A global deposit-return reusable packaging platform, founded by TerraCycle, that scaled commercially in France while most of its pilot markets — including the US, UK and Canada — failed to move beyond trial stage.

## Organisation Overview

Loop is a reusable-packaging platform conceived by TerraCycle and launched publicly at the World Economic Forum's Davos meeting in January 2019, with pilots beginning in Paris and New York that May. It lets consumer-goods brands sell products in durable, deposit-bearing containers (glass, steel, or engineered plastics) that customers return for cleaning and refilling rather than disposal. Backed at launch by dozens of major CPG companies, Loop has since narrowed sharply in scope: as of late 2025 it is commercially established only in France, with a smaller but growing footprint in Japan, while its US, UK and Canadian retail trials did not achieve commercial scale.

## Business Model

Loop operates a business-to-business-to-consumer model: it licenses reusable, deposit-bearing packaging designs and provides reverse-logistics infrastructure (collection, cleaning, refilling, redistribution) that consumer-goods brands and retailers plug into, rather than selling directly as a standalone retailer for most of its history. Brands pay to package selected products (food, beverages, household cleaning, personal care) in Loop-compatible durable containers; retailers stock these products alongside standard versions; consumers pay a refundable deposit on the container, which is redeemed on return via in-store drop-off points. Loop originally launched as a standalone e-commerce operation (2019) but by 2022–2023 had shifted its emphasis to in-store retail integration, judging this more sustainable than direct-to-consumer shipping and returns. Commercial viability has proven highly dependent on regulation: in France, the 2020 AGEC anti-waste law (which sets a target of 10% reusable packaging placed on the market by 2027, among other reuse provisions) has driven major retailers (Carrefour, Monoprix, Système U) to commit shelf space to Loop products, whereas in markets without comparable mandates (the US, UK, Canada) retail partners reported that consumer demand did not translate into increased profitability, and pilots were not renewed.

## Products & Services

A licensed system of durable, reusable containers (glass, stainless steel, and engineered/recyclable plastics) for food, beverage, household cleaning, and personal-care products, plus the associated deposit-collection, cleaning, and refill infrastructure that brands and retailers use to offer these products at store level or via delivery.

## Customers

- Consumer-packaged-goods (CPG) brands that package selected products for the Loop system (e.g., Coca-Cola, Nestlé, Unilever, Procter & Gamble, Danone, Häagen-Dazs, Heinz, Nutella/Ferrero, Evian, Suntory, Jacobs Douwe Egberts)
- Retailers that stock Loop products in-store (e.g., Carrefour, Monoprix and Système U in France; Aeon, Askul and StyleTable in Japan)
- End consumers who purchase Loop-packaged products and return containers for a deposit refund

## Measurable Impact

- France: 250+ participating stores across three retailers (Carrefour, Monoprix, Système U) as of TerraCycle's 2024 Year in Review (terracycle.com, published 2025)
- Japan: 110+ participating stores across three retailers (Aeon, Askul, StyleTable) as of the same 2024 review
- Carrefour stocks approximately 40 Loop returnable products (Trellis, December 2025)
- As of end-2022, Loop reported roughly 150 active retail locations worldwide, including about 36 in the US, and stated that roughly two-thirds of participating consumers returned containers two or more times across all markets (Waste Dive, 2023)
- ADEME's (France) National Reuse Observatory reported 2.75 billion reusable packaging units placed on the French market in 2024 — 1.82% of all packaging placed on the market that year, against an AGEC-law target of 10% reusable by 2027 (industry press, 2025). This is a national/regulatory figure, not a Loop-specific metric, but provides context for the regulatory environment Loop operates in.

No independently audited unit, tonnage, or emissions-reduction figures specific to Loop were found. All figures above are self-reported (TerraCycle) or drawn from trade press citing company/retailer statements.

## Challenges

Loop's public trajectory since 2019 has been widely reported as a story of retrenchment. Fast Company (2025) characterised the venture as having "failed—except in France," quoting founder Tom Szaky: "Consumers liked it. They bought, they returned... But we couldn't get [retailers] anywhere except France." Reported reasons include: retailers found that consumer enthusiasm for reusable packaging did not translate into increased sales or profitability sufficient to justify scaling logistics investment; the standalone e-commerce/home-delivery model proved operationally difficult and was largely phased out by 2022–2023 in favour of in-store retail integration; and, critically, most non-French markets lacked the regulatory mandates that exist in France under the AGEC anti-waste law. Named discontinued pilots include Tesco (UK, ended July 2022) and multiple US retail trials (Kroger/Fred Meyer, Giant Food, Walmart, Walgreens) that were not renewed or expanded to national scale. TerraCycle's own commentary (via Trellis, December 2025) indicates future expansion will be targeted specifically at markets developing comparable reuse regulation — the UK, Iberia, and Benelux were named as the most likely next markets — rather than broad-based rollout.

## REUSE Foundation Assessment

Loop remains one of the highest-profile branded attempts at a multinational deposit-return reusable-packaging system, with genuine, verifiable commercial scale in France (250+ stores, major CPG brand participation) and a smaller but real footprint in Japan. Its documented failure to scale in the US, UK, and Canada is itself a valuable, well-sourced case study for the sector: it demonstrates that consumer willingness to participate in reuse systems does not by itself drive retailer adoption absent regulatory mandates (as in France's AGEC law) or a clear profitability case. This makes Loop instructive both as a positive example (France) and a cautionary one (elsewhere), which is unusual and valuable for a knowledge base focused on real-world reuse economics rather than aspirational pilots.

**Priority Rating: ★★★★ High** — a globally recognised, well-documented deposit-return venture with verifiable commercial scale in at least one major market and rich, well-sourced material on both successes and failures; not rated Essential/5-star because current verified operational scale is now geographically narrow (essentially France, with a secondary Japan presence) rather than the broad multinational footprint implied by the original seed data.
