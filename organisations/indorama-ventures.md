## Organisation Overview

Indorama Ventures is a Bangkok-headquartered global petrochemicals group founded in 1994, publicly listed on the Stock Exchange of Thailand since 2008. It is the world's largest producer of PET (polyethylene terephthalate) resin and describes itself as the world's largest producer of recycled PET (rPET) for beverage bottles. The company operates over 100 manufacturing sites across more than 30 countries and reports around 28,000 employees (2024). Its business spans three core segments: Integrated Oxides and Derivatives, Combined PET, and Fibers, and includes dedicated PET recycling assets (e.g., acquired CarbonLite's Texas recycling facility).

## Business Model

Indorama Ventures is a large, vertically integrated petrochemicals manufacturer generating revenue by producing and selling PET resin, fibers, and intermediate chemicals to packaging, textile and other industrial customers worldwide. Its recycling business processes post-consumer PET bottles (collected via existing recycling infrastructure, not a company-run collection/reuse system) into recycled PET resin (rPET), which is then sold back into the packaging and textile supply chain as a substitute for virgin PET. The company has publicly touted milestones such as recycling 100 billion PET bottles cumulatively and partnerships to recycle billions of bottles per year. This is fundamentally a materials-recovery/recycling operation — breaking down collected plastic and reprocessing it into new resin — rather than a reuse model in which a package or product is reused in its original form; growth has come substantially through acquisitions of recycling and petrochemical assets globally.

## REUSE Foundation Assessment

As the seed brief itself flagged, Indorama Ventures should be assessed honestly: it is a materials-recycling giant, not a reuse company. Its core activity — melting and reprocessing collected PET into new resin — is downstream recycling, distinct from reuse models that keep a package, container or product in circulation in its original form. It is relevant to the broader circular economy and to REUSE Foundation's material-recovery context, but does not represent a reuse system, refill infrastructure, or reusable packaging pool, and should be catalogued as an adjacent/complementary player rather than a core reuse organisation.
