## Organisation Overview

EcoPost is a Kenyan social enterprise, founded in 2009 by Lorna Rutto (with co-founder Charles Kalama), that converts recycled plastic waste and agricultural biomass into durable "plastic lumber" building materials, most notably fencing posts, as an alternative to timber. Based in Nairobi, the company addresses urban plastic pollution, deforestation and youth unemployment simultaneously by purchasing plastic waste from waste pickers and collectors, giving informal waste workers an income source, and manufacturing long-lasting construction and landscaping products. Founder Lorna Rutto has received international recognition (including as a Schwab Foundation/CNN featured social entrepreneur) for the venture.

## Business Model

EcoPost operates a for-profit social-enterprise model that buys plastic waste (HDPE and PP) and agricultural/biomass residues from garbage collectors, waste pickers, sawmills and waste yards across the Nairobi area, providing income to informal waste-collection communities. The purchased material is processed into extruded "plastic lumber" — durable, weather-resistant boards and posts sold in various cross-sections and colours — which substitute for timber in fencing, landscaping, raised beds, pergolas, signage, benches, cattle enclosures, garages, shipping pallets, and other construction applications, thereby also reducing pressure on forests. Revenue comes from direct sales of these building products through the company's own outlets and a hardware-store retail channel, supplemented by corporate partnerships and programme funding (e.g., school-fencing initiatives and environmental-education programmes) from organisations including Coca-Cola, Safaricom Foundation, KCB Bank, WWF and Borealis. The company has also signalled plans to expand into textile recycling and fencing services.

## Products & Services

Recycled-plastic "plastic lumber" fencing posts and boards; cattle enclosures, garages, shipping pallets, feed troughs, flowerpots/planters, garbage receptacles, road signage, playground equipment, floor decking, boardwalks and truck flooring; school-fencing and environmental-education programmes.

## Customers

Residential, agricultural and institutional customers purchasing fencing and construction products; schools (via fencing initiatives); hardware retailers; corporate partners funding environmental/education programmes.

## Partnerships

Corporate/funding partners named on the official site: Coca-Cola, KCB Bank, Safaricom Foundation, World Wildlife Fund (WWF), and Borealis (which the company states "supports Ecopost to drive circular economy in Kenya").

## Measurable Impact

Third-party coverage (not confirmed against a dated primary EcoPost impact report) states that over roughly 15 years EcoPost has recycled over 13 million kilograms of plastic waste, supported more than 300 direct jobs and approximately 12,000 indirect income opportunities in marginalised communities, helped preserve approximately 4,500 acres of forest, and mitigated over 160 million kilograms of CO2 emissions. These figures should be treated as approximate pending confirmation from a dated primary source.

## Technology

Plastic-lumber extrusion technology converting mixed recycled HDPE/PP plastic waste and biomass into durable, weather-resistant construction-grade lumber; no consumer-facing digital or reuse-tracking technology identified.

## REUSE Foundation Assessment

EcoPost is a well-established (2009), award-recognised Kenyan social enterprise with a genuinely circular material-recovery model that converts hard-to-manage mixed plastic waste into long-life durable goods, while also creating income for informal waste collectors and reducing deforestation. Its activity is more accurately categorised as material recovery/industrial reuse of plastic waste into new durable products rather than "reusable containers" in the sense of packaging reuse, but it remains a strong example of circular-economy value creation from plastic waste in an emerging-market context, with credible corporate partnerships (Coca-Cola, WWF, Safaricom, Borealis).
