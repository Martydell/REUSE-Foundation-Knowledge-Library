## Organisation Overview

The "Swappa Crate" is New Zealand's original and longest-running refill scheme, built around standardised, reusable glass beer bottles that originated with the Auckland Bottling Company (ABC) in 1920. At its 1975 peak, ABC-standard bottles made up roughly 92% of New Zealand's packaged beer market (about 336 million bottles annually). The scheme is now owned by brewers Lion and DB, operated logistically by Associated Bottlers Co., and promoted as an industry case study by the Glass Packaging Forum (GPF) — an accredited, industry-funded product stewardship scheme (part of The Packaging Forum, established 2006) rather than the operator itself. It is not a standalone company; it is an industry-wide reuse infrastructure system.

## Business Model

The scheme standardises beer bottle design across participating brewers, enabling any returned bottle to be washed and refilled by any participating bottler rather than requiring brand-specific bottle matching. Consumers return empty bottles (commonly in branded "Swappa" crates) via retail and hospitality return points; bottles are collected through existing beverage-distribution reverse logistics, inspected, washed, and refilled at bottling facilities around the country, with unsuitable bottles sent to recycling. Reported figures (via GPF and trade press) indicate approximately six million bottles circulate through the system annually, each bottle reused around four times a year and remaining in service for roughly 10 years (some up to 15). The system is funded and governed by the participating brewers and coordinated as an industry initiative rather than as a commercial reuse-as-a-service business.

## Products & Services

Standardised reusable glass beer bottles and "Swappa" crates; bottle washing and refilling infrastructure for participating breweries; nationwide return-point network via retail and hospitality.

## Measurable Impact

Reported by the Glass Packaging Forum and NZ trade press (figures are industry-reported, not independently audited, and vary between sources):

- Approximately six million bottles circulate through the system annually; each bottle is reused around four times per year and lasts roughly 10 years, some up to 15 (Glass Packaging Forum, glassforum.org.nz).
- Each refillable bottle is reported to prevent the use of "39 single-use glass bottles of a similar size over its lifetime," with refillable glass bottles cited as having 85% less emissions on average than single-use glass bottles (Glass Packaging Forum, glassforum.org.nz).
- A separate industry trade-press source (The Shout NZ) cites a different multiplier — one reusable bottle replacing "approximately 90" standard 330ml bottles over its lifecycle — and states that in 2020 the equivalent of 11 million 330ml bottles was avoided from landfill through the scheme. This figure conflicts with the Glass Packaging Forum's "39 bottles" figure; both are self/industry-reported and undated to a consistent methodology.
- The scheme grew 13% year-on-year from 2015 to the start of the COVID-19 pandemic (Glass Packaging Forum; The Shout NZ), and has reportedly regained lost volume since.

## Technology

Not publicly available — the scheme relies on standardised bottle/crate design and conventional beverage-distribution reverse logistics rather than any documented digital tracking (e.g., RFID or app-based) system.

## REUSE Foundation Assessment

The Swappa Crate system is one of the world's longest-running, large-scale refillable glass bottle schemes, with over a century of continuous operation and millions of bottles cycling through reuse annually — a strong, real-world proof point for reusable packaging at national scale. It should be catalogued and presented as an industry initiative under the Glass Packaging Forum / participating brewers (Lion, DB), not as a standalone company, consistent with the seed data's own framing. Its long history, national infrastructure, and documented (if imprecisely quantified) reuse volumes make it highly relevant to the REUSE Foundation's mission as a durable, non-digital reuse-infrastructure case study, though reported impact figures conflict between sources and should be treated as indicative rather than precise.

**Priority Rating: ★★★★ High** (large-scale, long-running national reusable-packaging infrastructure; core reuse scope; some impact-figure inconsistency between sources)
