## Organisation Overview

Jean Bouteille is a French company founded in Lille that pioneered a returnable-bottle, bulk-liquid dispensing system for retail stores. Named as a play on founder Gérard Bellet's name, the company provides in-store dispensing fixtures and reusable glass or plastic bottles so shoppers can buy liquid products (wine, oil, juice, detergent, shampoo, cleaning products) without single-use packaging. It has certified "Entreprise à Mission" and ESUS (social utility) status since 2018, reflecting its stated purpose of accompanying retailers and consumers through zero-waste transition.

## Business Model

Jean Bouteille sells and leases dispensing equipment (fountains/taps for liquids) plus reusable containers to retailers — a B2B "packaging-as-a-service"-style model layered onto in-store bulk retail. Consumers pay a small deposit (reported at roughly 1-2 euros) to take a reusable Jean Bouteille bottle, refill it as often as they like at partner stores, and can return it at any time. The bottles are designed for repeated reuse (sources cite 25+ reuse cycles). Revenue comes from equipment sales/installation to retail partners (reported across roughly 1,000-1,400 French points of sale), ongoing bottle and consumable supply, and consulting/support for retailers implementing bulk-liquid zero-waste offers. The company positions itself as an enabler for supermarkets, wine merchants, and independent shops wanting to add a bulk-liquid, reusable-packaging offer without building their own system from scratch, differentiating it from pure-play zero-waste retailers by focusing on the infrastructure and container side of the value chain.

## REUSE Foundation Assessment

Jean Bouteille is a well-documented, long-running (since 2012) French refill-systems company with a genuine mission-driven legal status (ESUS certification) and a proven retail deployment footprint. It is a strong, directly relevant example of B2B refill infrastructure for liquid products, though independent third-party impact verification (e.g., audited waste-reduction tonnage) was not found in public sources.
