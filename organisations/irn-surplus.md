## Organisation Overview

IRN (Institution Recycling Network), operating as "The Reuse Network," is a small US-based nonprofit-style organisation that connects institutions — schools, colleges, corporations, healthcare facilities, and government agencies — with surplus furniture and equipment to nonprofit organisations and communities in need. Originally a recycling cooperative for education and healthcare institutions, it pivoted toward furniture reuse after founder Mark Lennon observed usable furniture being discarded while people lacked basic furnishings. It is based in Concord, New Hampshire, and operates with a small staff (reportedly around 10 people) coordinating logistics between roughly 800 donor institutions and 300+ nonprofit recipients.

## Business Model

IRN operates a "charitable reuse" logistics and matching model rather than a retail or resale business. Institutions with surplus furniture (dorm furniture, office equipment, healthcare furnishings) contact or are contacted by IRN, which coordinates collection and shipping logistics to match that surplus with vetted nonprofit recipient organisations — including disaster relief groups, Habitat for Humanity ReStores, furniture banks, and local charity thrift shops — domestically and internationally. Revenue and sustainability sourcing (donor fees, grants, or service charges) is not detailed publicly. The process is described as relationship-based and staff-coordinated rather than powered by a public digital matching platform. IRN frames its work as delivering value on three fronts simultaneously: environmental (landfill diversion), social (furnishing under-resourced communities and disaster relief efforts), and economic (institutions avoid disposal costs). It publishes periodic "impact" blog updates (e.g., annual totals) summarizing tonnage diverted and items distributed.

## Circular Economy Activities

- Reverse Logistics (collection and shipping coordination of surplus institutional goods)
- Take-back (institutions surrender surplus furniture/equipment for redistribution)
- Waste Reduction (landfill diversion of furniture and equipment)
- Sharing (redistribution of donated surplus to nonprofits/communities in need)

## Products & Services

Surplus furniture and equipment collection, logistics coordination, and matching/redistribution services connecting institutional donors with nonprofit recipient organisations; no direct-to-consumer retail offering.

## Customers

Donor side: approximately 800 institutions, including K-12 schools, colleges/universities, corporations, government agencies, and healthcare facilities. Recipient side: 300+ nonprofit organisations across roughly 43–46 US states and 62–67 countries (figures vary slightly by publication date), including disaster relief and economic development programs.

## Partnerships

- Habitat for Humanity ReStores (redistribution partner, per company materials)

## Measurable Impact

- Reported to have surpassed 2 million furniture items distributed to charity (company press release, August 2025 per irnsurplus.com blog dating).
- 100+ million pounds of furniture/equipment diverted from US landfills (company-reported).
- Approximately 2,500 reuse projects completed through 2023 (company-reported).
- Operating reach cited as 43–46 US states and 62–67 countries (figures vary by source/date).
- All figures are company self-reported via its own website/blog; no independent third-party audit of these metrics was found.

## Technology

No public-facing digital matching platform or app was identified; the redistribution process is described in company materials as relationship-based and coordinated directly by IRN staff via phone/email outreach and logistics coordination rather than an automated or IoT-based system.

## REUSE Foundation Assessment

IRN/The Reuse Network is a long-running, niche but credible player in institutional surplus reuse, with a clear and consistently reported mission (charitable furniture redistribution) and multi-decade track record. Its activities map directly onto reverse logistics, take-back, and waste-reduction categories central to the REUSE Foundation's interest areas. However, public verification is limited: founding-year sourcing is inconsistent, explicit nonprofit/501(c)(3) status was not confirmable from the sources reviewed, and all impact metrics are self-reported without independent audit. It is a small organisation (~10 staff) with modest public documentation compared to larger circular-economy actors, but its niche focus and multi-decade operating history are noteworthy.
