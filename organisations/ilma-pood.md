## Organisation Overview

Ilma Pood ("store without" in Estonian) opened in Tallinn in 2020 as one of Estonia's first package-free/zero-waste retail concepts. It sold dry foods, cosmetics and household products in bulk, encouraging customers to bring their own reusable containers, and it ran a "Can Bank" where customers could borrow donated containers if they didn't have their own. A second, crowdfunded location opened in Tartu. The business marked its fifth anniversary around 2025, framed publicly as a closing celebration, and available registry data indicates the company has since wound down operationally.

## Business Model

Ilma Pood operated as a physical retail shop selling unpackaged dry goods (grains, cereals, nuts, spices), liquid products, cosmetics and household cleaning items by weight/volume, sourced primarily from Estonian and Latvian producers with an emphasis on ethical, local supply chains. Customers purchased using their own reusable containers, or borrowed one from the shop's community-stocked "Can Bank," reducing single-use packaging at the point of sale. Revenue came from direct retail sales at both a Tallinn flagship and a Tartu location. Estonian company registry data shows revenue of roughly €82,500 in 2024 declining sharply toward 2025–2026, alongside unpaid VAT, social tax and income tax obligations from 2025, a shrinking headcount (from a peak of 6 employees in 2022 to 0 by Q2 2026), and a January 2026 consumer-protection ruling finding the company had failed to deliver purchased goods and had ignored customer complaints — all indicating the retail operation has effectively ceased.

## REUSE Foundation Assessment

Ilma Pood is a small but historically notable pioneer — one of Estonia's first zero-waste refill shops, with a community container-sharing scheme. However, current registry evidence (zero employees since early 2026, unresolved tax debts, a negative consumer-protection ruling, and revenue forecast to zero) strongly suggests the business has ceased or is ceasing normal operations as of this research date. Given its small scale even at peak and its now-defunct status, its current relevance to the REUSE Foundation's active organisational tracking is limited, though it retains historical/case-study value as an early refill-store model.
