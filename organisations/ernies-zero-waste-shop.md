## Organisation Overview

Ernie's Zero Waste Shop is an independent refill and zero-waste retailer based in Norwich, England, founded by Graham Rutherford in 2019 (originally trading as "Ethical Ernie's"). Now located at 62 Castle Quarter, Norwich, the shop stocks over 900 plastic-free, plant-based, and cruelty-free products, including bulk dry food and liquid refills for cleaning and personal-care items. It describes itself as Norwich's dedicated zero-waste shop, positioning itself as a local, community-facing alternative to conventional packaged retail.

## Business Model

Ernie's operates a bricks-and-mortar refill shop where customers bring their own containers (or purchase reusable ones in-store) to fill with bulk dry goods — cereals, nuts, dried fruit, pasta, rice, lentils, spices, tea and coffee — and liquid products such as laundry liquid, washing-up liquid, fabric conditioner, shampoo, conditioner, and hand/body soap, priced by weight or volume. The shop also sells packaged plastic-free alternatives (bamboo toothbrushes, reusable wipes, metal razors, stainless steel bottles) that are not part of the refill system. Revenue comes from direct in-store retail sales plus click & collect and local/UK-wide delivery (free UK delivery over £50; local delivery £2.99 for NR1–NR7 postcodes). The core reuse model is a "bring your own container" closed loop for liquids and a "buy only what you need" bulk model for dry goods, both intended to eliminate single-use packaging at the point of sale. As a small independent retailer, no external investment, franchising, or wholesale/B2B activity has been identified in public sources.

## REUSE Foundation Assessment

Ernie's Zero Waste Shop is a small, independent, single-location refill retailer representative of the grassroots "zero waste shop" movement in the UK. Its core activity — enabling customers to refill containers for both dry and liquid goods rather than buying pre-packaged single-use items — is directly relevant to reuse and packaging-waste-reduction goals, and its self-reported bottle-saving counter, while unaudited, demonstrates sustained community engagement since 2019. However, it has limited scale (one shop, one city), no franchising or replication model, no publicly disclosed corporate partnerships, and no digital tracking or measurement infrastructure, which limits its value as a model for wider systemic replication. It remains a useful case study of community-level refill retail rather than a scalable reuse infrastructure provider.
