## Organisation Overview

joni is a Canadian period-care company founded in March 2020 by co-founders Linda Biggs and Jayesh Vekariya, headquartered in Victoria, British Columbia. It is a B Corp-certified company selling organic, plant-based, biodegradable and compostable pads, tampons and period underwear, aiming to eliminate conventional plastic from menstrual products (which can otherwise take over 300 years to break down). Beyond direct-to-consumer sales, joni supplies over 450 Canadian retailers and offers "Model V," a free-vend period-care dispenser for workplaces, schools and public facilities, alongside a stated commitment of at least 2% of revenue toward distributing free period products to those experiencing period poverty.

## Business Model

joni's core commercial model is direct-to-consumer e-commerce and retail distribution (450+ retail points in Canada and the US) of individually wrapped, plant-based pads and tampons, whose packaging is certified compostable (BPI and ok-compost by TÜV standards) rather than reusable — products themselves are single-use, biodegrading on average 94% within 12 months under standard composting conditions. Its secondary, B2B commercial line is the "Model V" smart free-vend dispenser, reusable hardware installed in workplaces, schools and public facilities that dispenses joni's compostable products on demand, positioning the company as an accessible period-care infrastructure provider rather than purely a packaged-goods brand. The company has grown rapidly — recognised in November 2025 as one of Canada's top 20 fastest-growing companies — and channels part of its revenue and product into philanthropic distribution through nonprofit partners addressing period poverty.

## REUSE Foundation Assessment

Get Joni should be assessed honestly against the REUSE Foundation's reuse-economy focus: its core product line is compostable, biodegradable, single-use menstrual care — a genuine and well-certified sustainable-materials innovation, but not "reuse" in the sense of packaging or products used repeatedly. The one component with a plausible reuse-economy tie is the "Model V" dispenser, which is reusable hardware infrastructure providing repeated, on-demand access to (still single-use) products — closer to a packaging-as-a-service/dispensing-infrastructure model than to reusable packaging itself. The company is credible, growing rapidly, and B Corp-certified with real philanthropic reach, but its relevance to a reuse-focused knowledge library is limited and should not be overstated.

Priority: ★★ Low (credible, fast-growing compostable single-use brand; only the Model V dispenser hardware has a meaningful, narrow tie to the reuse economy)
