## Organisation Overview

Ekoplaza is the largest organic supermarket chain in the Benelux region, tracing its roots to a single Amsterdam organic shop opened by Gerard Does in 1980; the modern Ekoplaza supermarket formula itself was developed by Jos Kamphuys, whose first trial store opened in Alkmaar in April 2005. Following Kamphuys' death in 2011, parent company Udea B.V. became full owner. By 2018 the chain operated around 74 stores in the Netherlands, and it has since expanded into Belgium. Ekoplaza is best known internationally for launching the world's first supermarket "plastic-free aisle" in an Amsterdam store in February 2018.

## Business Model

Ekoplaza operates as a franchise-supported organic supermarket chain, with parent company Udea B.V. providing wholesale distribution, logistics and franchise-formula support to individual store operators across the Netherlands and Belgium. Stores sell a full organic grocery range — fresh produce, dairy, meat, bakery, pantry staples — differentiated from conventional supermarkets by exclusively organic sourcing and, since 2018, dedicated "plastic-free" sections. The plastic-free aisle initiative, developed as "Ekoplaza Lab" in partnership with UK environmental group A Plastic Planet, replaced conventional plastic packaging on over 700 products with compostable bioplastics, glass, metal and cardboard alternatives, with an initial plan to extend the concept chain-wide. Revenue derives from standard organic-grocery retail margins; the plastic-free program functions primarily as packaging innovation and public positioning rather than a deposit or refill system.

## REUSE Foundation Assessment

Ekoplaza is a significant, well-documented mainstream retailer notable for a genuinely first-of-kind packaging innovation (the 2018 plastic-free aisle), giving it strong media/public visibility relevant to circular-economy narratives. However, its core activity is organic-food retail rather than reuse infrastructure — the plastic-free aisle substitutes compostable/recyclable materials for plastic rather than establishing reusable packaging or refill/deposit loops, which limits its direct relevance to the REUSE Foundation's reuse-specific mandate. Useful as a packaging-innovation case study rather than a reuse-system exemplar.
