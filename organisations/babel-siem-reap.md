## Organisation Overview

Babel Siem Reap is an independently owned eco-focused guesthouse and boutique hotel in Siem Reap, Cambodia, operating since 2008. As part of its sustainability positioning, Babel runs an on-site "Eco-shop and refill station" in its lobby, described by the property as the country's first zero-waste shop and refill station of its kind. As noted in the seed data, this is a hotel/guesthouse sustainability initiative embedded within a hospitality business, not a standalone reuse company, and is presented here accordingly.

## Business Model

Babel's core business is guesthouse and boutique-hotel accommodation for travellers in Siem Reap, generating revenue primarily from room bookings. Layered onto this hospitality business is a retail sustainability initiative: guests and visitors can buy a reusable water bottle at Babel's eco-shop and refill it for free with water on-site, then continue refilling it at other participating stations around Siem Reap and, via the regional "Refill NOT Landfill" campaign and its associated station map (refillasia.com/map), across several other Southeast Asian countries. The eco-shop also stocks locally made, plastic-free lifestyle products — for example tote bags produced by Friends N Stuff, a local employment initiative — giving travellers plastic-free alternatives to disposable amenities. Babel states a broader commitment to community-benefit tourism, including hiring only local staff, drivers and guides, and sponsoring staff education. No detailed public information was found describing hair/body-product-specific refill dispensing (e.g. shampoo, soap) distinct from the water-bottle refill and general eco-shop retail offering referenced in the seed data.

## REUSE Foundation Assessment

Babel Siem Reap is a small but genuine example of a hospitality business embedding refill infrastructure into its operations and helping extend a regional refill-station network to travellers — a useful, low-cost model that other guesthouses/hotels in tourism-dependent economies could replicate. Its relevance to the REUSE Foundation library is real but limited in scale: it is one guesthouse's in-house initiative rather than a scalable or replicable reuse business model in its own right, and no independent impact data could be verified. It is best catalogued as a hospitality-sector case study of refill-station participation rather than a core reuse-industry organisation.
