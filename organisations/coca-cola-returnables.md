# Coca-Cola Returnables (Brazil)

Multi-decade returnable-bottle program run by The Coca-Cola Company's Brazilian bottling partners, offering refillable glass and PET bottles that customers exchange and pay for by liquid content only.

## Organisation Overview

"Retornáveis" is Coca-Cola's returnable-bottle packaging initiative in Brazil, under which consumers buy beverages in glass or "RefPET" (returnable PET) bottles, return the empty bottle at point of sale, and pay only for the liquid content on subsequent purchases. It is not a separate company but a packaging and marketing program operated by The Coca-Cola Company's Brazilian trademark licensee bottlers. Returnable formats are offered in glass (200ml, 290ml, 1000ml, 1250ml) and RefPET (1500ml, 2000ml) across Coca-Cola, Coca-Cola Zero, Sprite, Fanta, Del Valle, Guaraná Jesus, Kuat and Tuchaua brands, supported by periodic national promotional campaigns.

## Business Model

The returnable-bottle model is a core packaging strategy for Coca-Cola's Brazilian operations, not a separately monetized business. Bottling partners (regional franchisees of The Coca-Cola Company such as Coca-Cola FEMSA Brasil and Solar Coca-Cola) manufacture, fill, distribute, collect and re-fill glass and RefPET returnable bottles, which are sold through small retailers ("padarias"/"mercadinhos"), bars, restaurants and increasingly supermarkets. The economic proposition to consumers is a lower per-unit price after the first purchase, since only the liquid — not the packaging — is paid for on subsequent purchases; retailers act as the collection point, returning empty bottles into the bottler's logistics network for washing/sanitizing and refilling. According to industry sources, returnable PET bottles can be refilled roughly 25 times and returnable glass bottles up to 50 times before being retired from circulation. Returnable packaging represented approximately 20% of Coca-Cola's sales volume in Brazil and roughly 34% across the wider Latin America region, per company/industry statements reported in trade press. The program supports The Coca-Cola Company's global "World Without Waste" packaging strategy (announced 2018), under which the company targeted at least 25% of beverage volume sold in reusable/refillable packaging by 2030 — a target industry reporting notes was later removed from parts of the company's public communications.

## Circular Economy Activities

- Reusable Packaging — glass and RefPET returnable bottles, refilled up to ~25 (PET) or ~50 (glass) times
- Deposit Return — informal deposit-style economics: consumers pay full price with bottle first time, then liquid-only price on bottle return
- Reverse Logistics — bottler-run collection, cleaning and refilling infrastructure through retail points of sale
- Consumer Education — "Vai de Retornável" national promotional campaign encouraging returnable-bottle adoption
- Material Recovery — related SustentaPET initiative (Coca-Cola FEMSA Brasil, since 2019) for PET collection and recycling

## Products & Services

Returnable glass bottles (200ml, 290ml, 1000ml, 1250ml) and returnable "RefPET" plastic bottles (1500ml, 2000ml) across Coca-Cola, Coca-Cola Zero Sugar, Sprite, Fanta, Del Valle, Guaraná Jesus, Kuat and Tuchaua brands; associated "Promo Retornável" consumer rewards promotion (WhatsApp/website code registration for prizes).

## Customers

Brazilian consumers purchasing at small independent retailers, bars/restaurants, and (more recently) some supermarkets; the returnable format targets price-sensitive, high-frequency purchase occasions.

## Partnerships

Implemented through The Coca-Cola Company's licensed Brazilian bottling partners, reported in press coverage to include Coca-Cola FEMSA Brasil and Solar Coca-Cola (regional bottler brands such as Refrescos Bandeirantes, Brasal Refrigerantes and Sorocaba Refrescos also market the campaign in their territories). No further named third-party partners (e.g., NGOs, technology providers) were identified in public sources.

## Measurable Impact

- Returnable packaging reported at ~20% of Coca-Cola's total sales volume in Brazil, vs. ~34% across Latin America overall (trade press, 2024, citing company/industry data) — source: ad-hoc-news.de / industry reporting, not an audited company disclosure
- SustentaPET (Coca-Cola FEMSA Brasil, since 2019) reported to have recycled more than 3 billion PET bottles as of reporting reviewed — this figure is for the associated recycling program, not the returnable-bottle reuse program specifically
- Industry reporting states The Coca-Cola Company's 2018 "World Without Waste" pledge (25% reusable/refillable packaging by 2030) was later scaled back or removed from parts of the company's public communications (Fast Company Brasil, reporting a change to the commitment's public framing) — no updated global or Brazil-specific reuse percentage target was found to replace it in verified sources.

## Technology used

Not publicly available for bottle tracking (no RFID/QR/app system specific to the returnable-bottle logistics was identified). The associated "Promo Retornável" consumer promotion uses WhatsApp and web-based code registration for prize redemption.

## Challenges

Trade press (Fast Company Brasil) reported that in late 2023/2024 The Coca-Cola Company removed or altered public reuse and virgin-plastic-reduction commitment language from parts of its "World Without Waste" web materials, raising questions about the durability of its global reusable-packaging targets. No Brazil-specific operational challenges (e.g., collection logistics, contamination, retailer participation rates) were documented in the sources reviewed.

## REUSE Foundation Assessment

Coca-Cola's Brazilian returnable-bottle program is one of the largest reuse-packaging systems operating anywhere, built on decades of established bottle-return infrastructure through small retailers, and represents a meaningful share (~20%) of Coca-Cola's Brazil sales volume. It is a genuine reuse model — bottles are physically returned, washed and refilled dozens of times — rather than single-use recycling. However, it is a packaging initiative embedded within a multinational corporation's broader commercial strategy rather than a dedicated reuse organisation, its independently audited impact data is limited, and its parent company's global reuse commitments have reportedly softened in public communications. Its scale and legacy infrastructure make it highly relevant as a case study in mainstream FMCG reuse at national scale.
