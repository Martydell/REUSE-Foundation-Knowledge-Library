A Toronto-founded, now online-only Canadian retailer of low-waste body care, home care and food products sold via refill (bring-your-own-container) and a returnable-container lending programme.

## Organisation Overview

bare market is a Canadian low-waste retailer founded in 2018 by Dayna Stein, who was inspired by Vancouver's The Soap Dispensary and her graduate research on food-waste mitigation. The business began as a series of farmers'-market stalls and pop-ups before opening a permanent storefront on Danforth Avenue in Toronto in January 2020. That physical location closed on May 19, 2024, after which bare market transitioned to an online, delivery-based model, partnering with several small east-Toronto businesses for fulfillment. It markets itself as "Canada's Online Shop for Low Waste Goods + Refills."

## Business Model

bare market operates a direct-to-consumer e-commerce model selling body care, home care, DIY ingredient and non-perishable food products, with an emphasis on refillable and package-free formats. Customers can either bring their own containers (BYOC) for in-person refills (historically at the physical store, and now via any remaining local pickup points) or participate in a "bare lending" programme, borrowing reusable containers for a small, fully refundable deposit that is returned once the container comes back. The company ships Canada-wide and offers local delivery in the Greater Toronto Area. Since closing its flagship retail location in 2024, the company has shifted toward an online-first model supported by a small network of partner businesses rather than its own physical footprint, which represents a meaningful change from the original single-location bulk-refill store format.

## REUSE Foundation Assessment

bare market is a small, founder-led Canadian reuse retailer with a genuine multi-year track record (2018–present) spanning pop-up, physical-store and now online-only phases. Its BYOC refill model and reusable-container lending programme are consistent, low-tech reuse mechanisms, but the 2024 closure of its only physical retail location — the core venue for in-person refilling — significantly reduces its current reuse infrastructure and scale. No quantified impact data is publicly available, limiting assessment of real-world diversion achieved. It remains a credible example of a small-scale, community-oriented refill business but currently operates at a modest scale within Canada's low-waste retail sector.
