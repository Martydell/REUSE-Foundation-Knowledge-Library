## Organisation Overview
Grove Collaborative was founded in 2012 in San Francisco by Stuart Landesberg, Chris Clark and Jordan Savage as ePantry, an online subscription retailer for household staples, rebranding as Grove in 2016. It is a Certified B Corporation and Delaware public benefit corporation that sells natural personal-care, cleaning and household products via a direct-to-consumer subscription e-commerce model. The company went public via a SPAC merger with a Virgin Group-backed acquisition vehicle, completing its NYSE listing (ticker GROV) in June 2022. Grove markets itself as the "world's first plastic neutral retailer."

## Business Model
Grove Collaborative operates a curated e-commerce subscription platform selling natural household, cleaning, and personal-care products — both third-party sustainable brands and its own private-label lines — with recurring "VIP" subscription orders as its primary revenue driver. Its sustainability positioning centers on plastic reduction: the company set (and has since updated) public commitments to plastic neutrality and to avoiding cumulative single-use plastic waste (a stated goal of 15 million pounds avoided by 2030), and it applies on-site digital badges ("100% Plastic Free," "Reduced Plastic Waste," "No Single Use Plastic") to help customers shop by sustainability criteria. Recent initiatives include a partnership with the Oceanic Preservation Society launching "The Unplastic Shop," a curated collection aimed at reducing everyday plastic exposure. As a listed company (NYSE: GROV) it publishes annual sustainability reporting and has stated science-based emissions targets.

## REUSE Foundation Assessment
Grove Collaborative is a large, publicly listed retailer whose core business model is conventional e-commerce retail rather than a reuse system per se — it does not operate refill, deposit, or take-back infrastructure at meaningful scale. Its relevance to reuse rests on curation/consumer-education (steering purchases toward plastic-free and reusable-packaging products) and public plastic-reduction commitments with quantified 2030 targets, which is valuable as a demand-side signal but represents adjacent rather than core reuse-system activity.
